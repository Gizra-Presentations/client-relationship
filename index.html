There are two types of I - It realtionships

  • Those that are utilitarian.
  • Truncated versions of what should be deep relationships

Know the person and let the person know you.

What it means in practice

  • Ask questions unrelated to the project.
  • Know their history and the story of how they got to where they are now.
  • Understand the day-to-day demands, constraints, and opportunities in their position..

What it does

Every story is an entry point for understanding how we can help better. By fully immersing in the "other" we have a better sense of when to approach, and when to step back; when we can be a real asset, and when we are just a burden - another person to say no to.

Keep conversation going.

What it means in practice

  • Have a regular meeting schedule and keep it even if there is nothing to report.
  • Curate content that may be interesting and forward at regular intervals.
  • Keep the issue queue full of issues to discuss at a later date.

What it does

If we are always talking keeping the conversation going, we are more likely to get to the "important, not urgent" issues. It makes the relationship more about ongoing support than critical intervention.
